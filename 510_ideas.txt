Your existing search is keyword-based (?search= query param matching question/answer text). An embedding-based approach would transform this.

Public deck cards form your shared knowledge corpus.
Idea: Semantic Search. Replace the current string-match search with cosine similarity retrieval. A student searching "how to handle concurrent writes" would find cards about mutex locks, optimistic locking, CRDTs, and database isolation levels — even if none of those exact words appear in the query. This directly applies top-k retrieval from your IR class.
Idea: RAG-Powered AI Tutor. This is the flagship feature. Build a chat interface where students can ask questions like "Explain the tradeoffs between consistent hashing and range partitioning." The system retrieves the top-k most relevant cards from the embedding index (prioritizing the student's own cards + public deck cards), feeds them as context to an LLM, and generates a grounded answer that references specific cards the student can review. This closes the loop between passive reading and active studying — the tutor doesn't just answer, it points back to the student's own materials.

Pillar 3: Neuroscience-Based Learning Techniques
Your EOD revision is a great start, but the research literature points to much more powerful scheduling and practice strategies.
Idea: Spaced Repetition Scheduler (SM-2 or FSRS). Add review metadata to each flashcard: easeFactor, interval, repetitions, nextReviewDate, and a quality rating (0-5) after each review. The FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler) algorithm is the current state-of-the-art — it models memory stability and retrievability to compute optimal review times. This replaces the binary "reviewed today / not reviewed" with a scientifically calibrated schedule. Each time a student reviews a card in study mode, they rate their recall, and the scheduler updates the next review date.
Idea: Active Recall with Adaptive Difficulty. Your test mode currently quizzes on any deck. Enhance it so the system preferentially surfaces cards the student is about to forget (based on the spaced repetition curve), and adapts the difficulty of questions. For DSA cards, this could mean first asking "what algorithm pattern does this use?" before asking "write the solution," based on the student's performance history.
Idea: Interleaving Practice. Research shows mixing topics during practice (rather than blocking by topic) improves long-term retention. Build a "Smart Mix" study mode that pulls cards from multiple decks, weighted by the knowledge graph's concept relationships and the spaced repetition scheduler's urgency scores. A student might get a sliding window problem, then a system design question about caching, then a behavioral question — all related through the underlying concept of "optimizing repeated access patterns."
Idea: Metacognitive Confidence Calibration. Before revealing the answer, ask the student to rate their confidence (1-5). Track the correlation between confidence and actual correctness over time. Show them a calibration curve on their dashboard — are they overconfident on certain topics? Underconfident on others? This directly implements desirable difficulty and metacognitive monitoring from the learning science literature, and gives students insight into their own knowledge gaps.
